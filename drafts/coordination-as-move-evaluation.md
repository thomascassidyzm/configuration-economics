# Coordination Is a Move-Evaluation Problem, Not a Pricing Problem

The framework keeps walking up to one question and stepping back: what coordinates an economy once labour is no longer the allocator and price no longer tells the whole truth? This sketch opens the design space rather than closing it — and makes one bold claim. Coordination for a generative economy is not the problem of finding the right *price*. It is the problem of distributed *move-evaluation* across many boards. A direction, not a mechanism; the honest parts kept honest.

## The question, sharpened

An economy has to do two things at once: decide *who gets access* to life-supporting configurations, and *aim collective attention* at the work worth doing. For most of history a single mechanism did both. Labour rationed access (you ate if you worked) and pointed attention (effort flowed to where it was paid). The two functions rode on one rail.

That rail is coming loose. Automation decouples production from labour; the composition of scarcity is shifting from things-made to sinks-and-substrate. So the mechanism that did both jobs is failing at both, and the instrument we reach for instead — the market price — was only ever doing one of them, on one board.

The question is therefore not *how do we price externalities better*. It is the deeper one underneath: **once labour is no longer the allocator, what coordinates a generative economy — what grants access, and what aims attention — without re-importing deprivation as the enforcer or a single score as the truth?**

## Why it can't be answered from inside

Standard economics cannot generate the answer, because its frame *is* the thing dissolving. Its account of value is an account of an allocation mechanism (`labour-as-allocator`): marginal-productivity wage theory and the labour theory of value both describe local properties of how access got rationed under a particular scarcity regime. Ask that frame "what should coordinate us when the regime changes?" and it can only answer in the regime's own terms — more labour, more price, more growth. You are asking the allocator to design its own succession.

And the market's signal has a known failure mode the framework already names. Price keeps score on one board (`displaced-costs`, the polychess reframe in `option-space-as-chess-moves`): GDP is the score on the financial board, while the watershed, the topsoil, the trust, and the climate take moves nobody counted. Worse, the signal drifts from its referent over time (`money-as-signal`): coordination continues on the number long after the number stops tracking the physical state. A better price is still a price — still one board, still drift-prone.

## The reframe

Here is the move. **A generative economy coordinates the way a chess game is played, not the way a market clears.**

A market-clearing model asks for one thing: the price at which the board balances. A chess game asks something else of every player — *is this move better or worse than that one, given the position I can see?* No player computes the whole tree. None needs a global valuation of the board. They evaluate moves locally and directionally, and that is enough to play well.

This is exactly the gift the framework already banked at the policy level (`option-space-as-chess-moves`): you cannot compute global option space ω(C), but you *can* evaluate Δω(move) directionally, and the consequential cases come out unambiguous even when the global measure stays open. The claim here is that the same operation, run by many agents at once, *is* the coordination mechanism — not a substitute for one. Coordination is distributed local move-evaluation across the boards each player can see, inside institutions whose job is two-fold: **widen the set of boards each player can see** (turn uncounted moves into counted ones), and **protect the substrate** (keep the game open). Markets become one board among several — a fast, useful one — not the arbiter that collapses the rest.

`coordination-wealth` is what makes this affordable: trust and shared understanding are exactly what let players read each other's boards cheaply, without metering every interaction. Coordination of this kind is not friction-free, but its friction falls as coherence rises.

## What any answer must satisfy

Six constraints, each inherited from a proposition, that any candidate mechanism has to clear. They do not pick the mechanism. They rule out most of what looks like one.

1. **Aim at Δω, not at a single-board score.** Reward moves that keep options open; stop rewarding motion on one board that forecloses the others. (`value-option-space`, `option-space-as-chess-moves`)
2. **Read many boards.** Internalise the currently-uncounted moves — not by collapsing them into one number, but by keeping the accounts plural and visible. (`displaced-costs`, polychess)
3. **Run on local, directional signals.** A global measure of value is open and may stay open; the mechanism cannot wait on it. It must work the way the move-rule works — comparison, not magnitude. (`option-space-measurability`)
4. **Grant participation without deprivation.** Decouple basic access from labour performance, so contribution can align with what actually opens options rather than with what satisfies an accounting proxy — and without re-installing scarcity as the enforcer. (`participation-limits`, `labour-as-allocator`)
5. **Keep the signal bound to the substrate.** Build the physical-accounting feedback that markets lack, so the coordinating signal cannot drift free of exergy, sinks, and biosphere integrity. (`money-as-signal`, `binding-constraint`)
6. **Refuse the foreclosing move.** Whatever else it does, it is no-regret: it keeps R_living open across the horizons major classes of life depend on. (`viable-objective`)

## The design space

Five axes, each with the worn-out default on the left and the live direction on the right. The work is in the right column.

- **Signal.** One scalar price → plural, non-collapsed accounts plus *directional foreclosure-flags* (a layer that says "this move closes a board," not "this is worth £X").
- **Attention.** Aimed by labour-gate or capital-return → aimed at the generative-but-invisible work the framework already values and the market already misses: care, maintenance, prevention, substrate-building (`care-as-configuration`, `prevention-over-repair`). The boldest single lever: **make the invisible generative work the work that gets resourced.**
- **Participation.** Rationed by deprivation → a participation floor decoupled from labour, so the allocator's collapse doesn't take subsistence down with it.
- **Feedback.** Market-only → physical-accounting loops the signal is bound to and cannot outrun.
- **Locus of computation.** Central optimisation → distributed local evaluation. The central planner hits the same non-computability wall as the global ω measure; the chess-engine move dodges it. This is not an aside — it is *why* coordination has to be move-evaluation and not a solved price.

## The attractors to avoid

Name them, because each is a comfortable place to fall and call it an answer.

- **The single-number trap.** Replacing GDP with one new index — a "wellbeing score," a "configuration score" — just relocates the legibility problem and re-collapses the boards. The framework already refuses a global ω magnitude for exactly this reason; a coordinating super-metric is the same error wearing a kinder face.
- **The central-planner trap.** Any coordinator that must *compute* the generative value of everything inherits the non-computability of global ω. Distributed directional evaluation is not a weaker version of central planning; it is the thing that works where central planning provably can't.
- **The legibility-decay trap.** Whatever you make legible to coordinate on, the illegible-but-vital begins to decay (`legibility-truth-tradeoff`). A real mechanism *protects* the partially-illegible rather than forcing it into the metric — which means some of coordination must run on trust and judgement, not measurement, by design.
- **The deprivation relapse.** Any scheme that quietly re-introduces scarcity-enforcement as its participation layer rebuilds the instability `participation-limits` predicts. Watch for it; it hides well.
- **One-board recapture.** The standing risk that the plural accounts get re-collapsed back into price because price is fast and legible. Resisting this is not a one-time design choice but a permanent maintenance cost — itself a thing coordination has to fund.

## What stays open

We are not naming the institutions. We are naming the *kind of thing* the answer has to be — distributed, directional, multi-board, participation-decoupled, substrate-bound — and refusing the false comforts that masquerade as solutions. That is not a hedge; it is the honest output of the move-rule applied to its own hardest case. The framework's claim is not "here is how to run the economy." It is: **coordination for a generative economy is a move-evaluation problem, and we have been trying to solve it as a pricing problem, which is why it keeps not solving.** Reframing the question that way is the move. Playing it out is the work that follows.
