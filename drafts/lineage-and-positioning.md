## Scaffold: `/lineage` page

### Opening (your voice)

Something like:

> This work is not the first to argue that economic accounting must respect physical constraint. A century of thinkers have said related things, often more empirically, sometimes more polemically, rarely in this form. What follows is an honest reading of the debts: who said what, what this work inherits, and where it tries to add something distinct.

Optional: acknowledge that the author came to these questions via physics (distinction physics) rather than economics, and that this lineage is being mapped *post hoc* rather than as the work's starting point. That's honest and defuses the "this just rediscovers Georgescu-Roegen" attack.

### Frederick Soddy (1926) — *Wealth, Virtual Wealth, and Debt*

Soddy was a Nobel-winning chemist who argued, decades before anyone else did it seriously, that money and physical wealth are different things and that conflating them is the root of recurring economic crisis.

- **What he established:** Money is a signal; physical wealth is what can be consumed. The divergence between them is an active source of dysfunction, not a curiosity.
- **What CE inherits:** The energy-accounting move itself. P1 (`energy-income-inheritance`) and P16 (`money-as-signal`) are direct intellectual descendants.
- **What CE adds:** Forward-looking value measure (Soddy was diagnosing error, not proposing alternative accounting); proposition format; 100 years of intervening context (ecological overshoot, climate, biodiversity).

### Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen (1971) — *The Entropy Law and the Economic Process*

The foundational work of thermodynamic economics. Georgescu-Roegen argued that the economic process is fundamentally dissipative and that conventional economics, by ignoring this, is physically incoherent.

- **What he established:** Material throughput is fundamentally dissipative; entropy is the correct accounting unit; neoclassical growth theory violates physical law.
- **What CE inherits:** Throughput-as-cost (P2), physical-foundationalism, the whole register of the argument.
- **What CE adds:** Forward-looking option space as positive value measure. Georgescu-Roegen's framework diagnoses; CE attempts construction. (Claim to stress-test: is this actually a difference, or is GR's framework already forward-looking in a way I'm not seeing? Worth checking.)

### Herman Daly (1977–present) — *Steady-State Economics*, *Ecological Economics*

Daly made Georgescu-Roegen's thermodynamic economics concrete: scale, distribution, allocation as three distinct problems; critique of Solow on substitutability; steady-state as alternative to growth.

- **What he established:** The Solow–Daly debate (is capital substitutable for natural resources? Daly: no, or only marginally). Scale vs allocation as separate questions.
- **What CE inherits:** The substitution-limits argument (P14); the critique of treating physical and financial capital as interchangeable; the ecological-economics baseline.
- **What CE adds:** Configuration as a distinct ontological category (Daly treats natural and manufactured capital; configuration cuts differently); link to Assembly Theory; the epistemic-status format primitive.

### Joseph Tainter (1988) — *The Collapse of Complex Societies*

Tainter's thesis: complexity has diminishing marginal returns. Societies invest in complexity to solve problems; at some point the returns decline; eventually maintenance cost exceeds benefit and the system simplifies abruptly.

- **What he established:** Complexity-maintenance as an ongoing cost; diminishing returns as a structural feature; historical pattern across many societies.
- **What CE inherits:** P7 (`complexity-maintenance`) is near-direct. The claim that late-stage systems experience rising overhead is Tainter.
- **What CE adds:** Framing within option-space / value theory rather than historical-comparative. Tainter tells you *when* systems collapse; CE attempts to say *what configurations persist* in the first place.

**Note:** Tainter's thesis is contested in historiography (Lens 2 flagged this). Worth acknowledging in the main essay.

### James C. Scott (1998) — *Seeing Like a State*

Scott's legibility critique: states and other high-modernist projects simplify local knowledge to make it legible, and in doing so destroy what made it work.

- **What he established:** The word "legibility" in this specific sense; the trade-off between observability and function.
- **What CE inherits:** P18 (`legibility-truth-tradeoff`) is direct. The phrase "over-legibility accelerates hidden loss" is essentially Scott translated into this framework.
- **What CE adds:** Physical-accounting frame. Scott is anthropological/political; CE attempts to locate the legibility problem in measurement theory itself.

### Marilyn Waring (1988) / Nancy Folbre / Feminist Economics

Waring's *Counting for Nothing* (re-released as *If Women Counted*) demolished GDP's treatment of unpaid care. Folbre has extended the argument for decades. Feminist economics as a whole has argued that the most economically essential activities are the ones most invisible to standard metrics.

- **What they established:** GDP systematically miscounts; care is productive; measurement shapes policy shapes allocation.
- **What CE inherits:** P8 (`care-as-configuration`) is a direct consequence of taking this tradition seriously. P5 (`participation-limits`) echoes the unconditional-participation argument.
- **What CE adds:** Physical grounding for care's value — not just "we should value care morally" but "care is configuration maintenance that preserves future option space." This is an attempt to give feminist-economic claims a physical (not merely moral) backing.

### Lee Cronin (2020s) — *Assembly Theory*

Cronin and collaborators developed Assembly Theory as a measure of molecular and structural complexity based on minimum construction steps. The Assembly Index is the specific quantity.

- **What they established:** Complexity as a physically measurable, construction-history-based quantity. A bridge between chemistry, biology, and information theory.
- **What CE inherits:** The idea that complexity is not vague but measurable in principle. The connection between construction history and what can be said to exist.
- **What CE differs on — and this is the key novelty claim:** Assembly Index is backward-looking (minimum steps to construct this object). Option space is forward-looking (configurations reachable from this state). They are dual measures in a precise sense: Assembly measures what had to happen for this to be; option space measures what can happen now that this is.

This is the specific novelty delta worth foregrounding. It's also an open research problem (`option-space-measurability`).

### W. Brian Arthur / Eric Beinhocker — Complexity Economics

Arthur's work on increasing returns, path dependence, and non-equilibrium; Beinhocker's *The Origin of Wealth* (2006) synthesising complexity economics for a general audience.

- **What they established:** The economy as an evolutionary, adaptive, non-equilibrium system. Wealth as encoded information / fit design.
- **What CE inherits:** Non-equilibrium framing; evolutionary dynamics of configurations.
- **What CE differs on:** Grounding. Complexity economics grounds in algorithmic/computational metaphors; CE grounds in physics (thermodynamics + distinctions). P21 (`configuration-not-information`) is a direct argument against the pure-information framing that complexity economics sometimes slides into.

### Jason Hickel / Tim Jackson — Degrowth / Post-Growth

Hickel's *Less Is More* (2020); Jackson's *Prosperity Without Growth* (2009/2017). Both argue that absolute decoupling of GDP from material throughput is empirically unsupported at the scale required, and that economic coordination must be rethought to function within planetary boundaries.

- **What they established:** The empirical case against green-growth optimism; the planetary-boundaries framing (Rockström et al.) as quantitative constraint.
- **What CE inherits:** Growth skepticism under physical constraint; the seriousness of absolute (not just relative) decoupling as a question.
- **What CE differs on:** Tone and structure. Degrowth literature is polemical, policy-oriented, political. CE is descriptive, physical, declines to prescribe. CE also foregrounds configuration quality as an alternative to throughput, where degrowth tends to emphasise reduction.

**Careful claim:** CE is not degrowth. Degrowth says *less*. CE says *configured differently*. Throughput goes down in the CE view, yes — but the primary move is redirecting what counts as value, not prescribing contraction. This distinction is worth making explicitly; Lens 6 flagged "this is degrowth in disguise" as an attack the work does not currently defend against.

## The Specific Delta — CE's Novelty Claims

From the above, what is genuinely new in CE:

1. **Forward-looking value measure (option space).** Prior work measures construction history (Assembly Index) or dissipation (throughput). CE attempts to measure what configurations *enable*. Current status: direction of measurement rather than measurement itself (see `option-space-measurability`).

2. **Epistemic-status as format primitive.** Propositions carry explicit status markers (established / derived / contested / open). This is a formal move: claims in this work carry their own epistemic vouchers. Not a content claim but a format claim.

3. **Integrated AI guide (Alexander).** The epistemic companion is not a feature, it is part of the contribution. A living work whose format adapts to reader resolution. Alexander knows the corpus's structure, not just the corpus.

4. **The ratio formulation of the viable objective** (durable flourishing per unit of bounded throughput, P22). This is a specific proposal, distinct from Daly's steady-state (which constrains throughput at a level) and Raworth's doughnut (which operates via two boundaries). Whether the ratio is operational is `contested` / depends on `option-space-measurability`.

## What CE does not claim

- Empirical validation. This is a working framework, not a measured theory.
- Policy prescription. The framework describes constraints, not actions.
- Completeness. Coordination mechanisms under constraint are explicitly `open`.
- Originality on the energy-accounting move. That goes back to Soddy, and through Georgescu-Roegen, Daly, and the whole ecological-economics tradition.

The strongest form of the novelty attack (from Lens 6):

> "Soddy 1926. Georgescu-Roegen 1971. Daly 1977. Tainter 1988. Hickel 2020. What is new here?"

The answer, staked:

> "The forward-looking measure (option space, currently open), the epistemic-format primitive, the integrated guide, and the specific ratio formulation of the viable objective. Everything else is inheritance — and the work owes it openly."
