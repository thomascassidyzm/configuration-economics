# Proposed proposition: labour-as-allocator

## Why now

P4 (`work-wrong-question`) handles the *forward* claim: as automation and configuration take over what labour used to do, tying access to labour generates performative activity rather than value. What P4 does not do is supply the historical reframing — it leaves "work as the source of value" intact as an earlier truth that has merely lapsed. A reader can accept P4 and still believe that, until recently, labour *was* the value-source and the wage-system *was* tracking it.

This draft proposes the prior claim: labour was never the source of value. It was the coordination machinery that scarcity required for allocating access to life-supporting configurations. The conflation of allocator and source was always a category error, but it remained invisible while the allocator was doing its job. As the composition of scarcity shifts — energy, materials, automation, AI — the mechanism becomes maladaptive: it no longer tracks the thing it once tracked even imperfectly.

The two propositions stand together. P4 says "stop asking the labour question going forward." This one says "the labour question was always misframed — and noticing that is what makes the forward move land." It is also the connective tissue between P3 (`value-option-space`), P5 (`participation-limits`) and P17 (`coordination-wealth`): if value is configuration and coordination is itself wealth, then labour-as-allocation-mechanism is a specific historical instance of coordination — one whose usefulness was bounded by the form of scarcity it solved for.

## Lineage notes (for Tom)

The claim has fragments scattered across several traditions. None of them states it in this form, but each contributes something the proposition draws on.

**Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944).** The closest precedent. Polanyi's "commodity fiction" argues that labour, land, and money are not produced for sale and therefore are not really commodities — treating them as such required a violent reorganisation of pre-market society. He frames labour as a *fictitious commodity*: a thing that has been forced into market form despite not having the structure that market goods have. This is adjacent to the present claim but not identical. Polanyi's argument is about commodification (treating labour *as if* it were a sellable thing), not specifically about labour as allocation-mechanism-mistaken-for-value-source. He is also more interested in the social damage of the fiction than in its accounting status. Honest summary: Polanyi makes labour's strangeness as a commodity legible; he does not make the specific allocator-vs-source distinction this proposition needs.

**David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011) and *Bullshit Jobs* (2018).** *Debt* documents that wage labour is historically unusual — most of human economic life ran on credit, obligation, gift, and kinship rather than on hours-for-coin exchange. *Bullshit Jobs* observes that a substantial fraction of contemporary paid work fails the practitioner's own "is this useful?" test, and reads this as ritual and discipline rather than productivity. Both feed the present claim: *Debt* by showing the wage system is not the default human arrangement, *Bullshit Jobs* by surfacing the symptom — work as access-ticket — that the present claim explains. Graeber does not, however, frame it as "labour is an allocator misread as a source." His framing is anthropological and political, not configurational. Honest summary: Graeber supplies the empirical texture; the present proposition supplies the accounting frame Graeber did not articulate.

**Marshall Sahlins, *Stone Age Economics* (1972), and the broader substantivist tradition.** The argument that "the economy" in pre-market societies is embedded in kinship, ritual, and ecological relation rather than being a separate sphere — and that allocation of life-supporting goods happens via mechanisms other than wage labour. This is solid ground: the historical record genuinely contains many allocators that are not labour-for-wages. Honest summary: this is supporting evidence that labour-as-allocator is one mechanism among several, not the universal default; it does not articulate the value-vs-allocation distinction itself.

**Herman Daly and the ecological-economics tradition.** Daly is firm that labour is not a source of production in the physical sense — labour is a *configurer* of throughput, and the throughput itself comes from low-entropy energy and materials. *Steady-State Economics* (1977) and later work argue against the labour-and-capital-only framing inherited from neoclassical economics on the grounds that it misses where production physically comes from. This is the closest thing to a physically-grounded version of the present claim. Honest summary: Daly's move denies labour is a *physical* source of value; the present claim goes a step further and asks what labour *was* doing, and answers: allocating access.

**Karl Marx, *Capital* (1867).** Marx's labour theory of value (LTV) is the opposing tradition the present claim explicitly contests. LTV holds that socially-necessary labour time is the substance of exchange value. The present claim says: no, that was an artefact of the allocation mechanism in operation under capitalism's scarcity regime; what looked like labour producing value was labour rationing access to value that originated in configuration under exergy flux. Honest summary: this proposition is incompatible with LTV in its strict form, but it accommodates much of what LTV correctly noticed (that wages track something systematic, not just bargaining power) by reinterpreting that systematic thing as the allocation function rather than the value-substance.

**Marginal-productivity theory of wages (J. B. Clark and the neoclassical synthesis).** The mainstream alternative: wages reflect the marginal product of labour. The present claim is less strictly opposed to this than it is to LTV: marginal productivity is a description of how the allocator behaves under competitive conditions, and the present proposition can grant that the allocator approximated marginal product under specific regimes. The disagreement is at a higher level — marginal productivity describes the *behaviour of the allocator*, not the *source of value*; treating the former as the latter is the slip the proposition flags.

**Bucky Fuller, *Operating Manual for Spaceship Earth* (1969) and related writings.** Fuller argued that as automation increased, the proper move was to "make every human a millionaire" by recognising that the wealth was being produced by accumulated knowledge and technology, not by labour. This is closer in spirit to the present claim than most other lineage, and it has the physical-grounding instinct, but it skips the analytical step of saying *what labour was doing* if not producing value. Honest summary: Fuller has the right intuition and the right reframing toward configuration; this proposition supplies the missing middle step.

**What none of the lineage gives.** None of these writers states, in this form: *labour was a coordination mechanism for distributing access under a particular scarcity regime, and the conflation of allocator and source was the unmarked move that made the wage system feel natural.* That formulation is the proposition's contribution. It is novel in framing but not in any of its constituent pieces — Polanyi, Graeber, Daly, and Fuller each have parts of it.

## Where it could go wrong (steelman against)

**Marginal-productivity theory has a real empirical record.** In competitive labour markets with reasonably mobile labour, wages do track marginal product to a non-trivial degree. The wage premium for skilled work, the responsiveness of employment to productivity shocks, and the cross-sectional correlation between wages and measured productivity are not nothing. A reader can grant that labour is an allocator and still hold that it allocates in a way that broadly tracks contribution — making the value-vs-allocation distinction matter less in practice than the proposition implies. *Response sketch:* the proposition can grant this. Marginal-productivity describes how the allocator behaves under specific conditions; the claim is that those conditions are dissolving, not that they never obtained. The interesting case is what happens to wage-as-signal when marginal product of human attention falls toward zero in domain after domain, which is the predictive content the proposition is willing to be tested on.

**The labour theory of value has a serious philosophical core, not just a political residue.** Marx's argument was that *something* about socially-necessary labour time is doing real explanatory work — not that physical exertion is the only source of useful things, which Marx never claimed. The Marxist tradition would hear the present proposition as missing the point that labour, under capitalism, is the form in which configuration-contribution is *organised and disciplined*, not merely the proxy by which access is rationed. *Response sketch:* the proposition is compatible with much of this. "Labour as the form in which configuration-contribution is organised under capitalism" and "labour as an allocator that approximated configuration-contribution under specific conditions" are nearby framings. The substantive disagreement is whether labour is constitutive of value or instrumental to its production-and-distribution; the proposition takes the latter view, and grants that the former view captured what was visible from inside the regime.

**Some work genuinely does maintain configurations and would not happen without the labour-access link.** A care worker keeping a hospice running, a maintenance engineer keeping a water system functional, a teacher building skill in students — these are real configuration-maintenance, not allocation theatre. If the wage link were cut without replacement, much of this work would stop. The proposition can read as dismissive of the work the allocator currently does call forth. *Response sketch:* the proposition does not deny that labour does configuration-maintenance work, nor that the current allocator currently calls forth some of it. It says the allocator is unreliable at this and getting worse — that much necessary care-and-maintenance work is *not* called forth by the wage system (unpaid care, parenting, ecological stewardship), and much that is called forth is performative. The replacement question — how to ensure necessary configuration-maintenance happens without the wage allocator — is real and unsolved, but it lives in P5 (`participation-limits`), not here. This proposition is descriptive, not prescriptive.

**The proposition risks erasing exploitation.** A reading that says "labour was never the source of value, it was just an allocator" can sound like it is denying that labour has been systematically under-compensated relative to what it produced — a core claim of labour movements for two centuries. If labour is just an allocator, the exploitation argument loses its physical anchor. *Response sketch:* the proposition does not require taking a position on exploitation. The historical fact that some workers received less than the configuration-value their attention contributed to producing remains true under either framing — it just gets described as "the allocator was rigged to under-credit certain contributors" rather than "surplus labour was extracted." This is a translation, not a denial. The political and moral weight of historical labour struggle survives the reframing intact. The proposition's silence on prescription is deliberate and is the right kind of silence — politics belongs in political work, not in the configuration accounting.

**The historical claim is too clean.** "Allocator and source coincided closely enough until recently" is a tidy narrative that the historical record probably will not support in detail. Pre-industrial labour allocation included serfdom, slavery, gendered unpaid work, and many other arrangements that did *not* track contribution to configuration even roughly. The proposition's implication that the wage system worked-well-enough-once-upon-a-time may be projecting an idealised post-Enlightenment wage market backward onto a much messier history. *Response sketch:* fair, and worth softening in the surface text. The claim does not require that the allocator ever tracked contribution *fairly* — only that it tracked it *closely enough to be confused with the source*. Even under serfdom and slavery, hours-of-bodily-effort and produced-configuration were tightly linked in a way they no longer are. The proposition is about the *category-confusion*, not about the *fairness*, of historical labour systems.

## Voice match check

Existing proposition (P4 `work-wrong-question`, opening lines of surface text):

> For a long time, work solved two problems at once. It produced what people needed, and it determined who was allowed access to those needs.
>
> As long as those functions aligned, tying survival to labour appeared both practical and fair.
>
> That alignment is loosening.

Proposed proposition (`labour-as-allocator`, opening lines of surface text):

> Labour has long been described as the source of economic value — the thing that turns raw nature into wealth, the substance that prices are supposed to track.
>
> A different reading is available. For most of human history, labour was the mechanism by which people were allocated access to life-supporting configurations under conditions of scarcity. It was the coordination machinery scarcity required, not the thing being coordinated.
>
> The two roles were rarely distinguished because, under those conditions, they coincided closely enough.

The two have the same shape: declarative opening, "different reading is available" register rather than confrontation, sentence-per-line cadence, no exhortation. Where P4 says *that alignment is loosening* and lets the reader feel the consequence, this draft says *the two roles were rarely distinguished because they coincided closely enough* and lets the reader notice the historical conflation themselves. Both leave the political conclusion off the page.

One stylistic worry: this proposition's surface is slightly more *argumentative* than P4's — P4 narrates a process the reader already half-knows, while this one needs to make a less-familiar move (labour was never the source). It runs the risk of feeling like it is *making a case* rather than *observing a structure*. If Tom finds the surface too rhetorically active, the fix is to lean further into description ("Two readings are available. One has been dominant. The other...") and trust the reader to follow without being walked through it.
